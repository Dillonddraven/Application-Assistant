## Subject line
Pattern: `<Role> at <Company> — <one specific match>`. Examples: "Cybersecurity Analyst at Soro — Graylog logging labs", "SOC Analyst — SAST reporting + incident playbook". <= 70 chars, no emoji.

## Persuasion principles

- **Quote one responsibility verbatim.** Pick the single most operationally specific bullet from their JD's responsibilities and put it in quotes in your opening. Hiring managers wrote those bullets; seeing their own language returned signals close reading.
- **Match it with one specific proof point.** Don't list everything you've ever done — pick the one project / bullet that maps cleanly to the quoted responsibility, and describe what you built and the outcome. Cite the source via `source_id`. If a number appears, it must appear verbatim in the cited source's `text` or `metrics_present`.
- **Reciprocity preview.** Offer one concrete artifact: "I can share the lab Markdown / a redacted report sample / the repo / a one-page writeup" — choose what fits the proof point. Do NOT promise a deliverable that doesn't already exist in the profile.
- **Time-bounded CTA, written as a peer not a supplicant.** "Worth 20 minutes next week to talk through it?" reads better than "I'd appreciate any consideration".
- **Soft fallback.** End with "or could you point me to the right contact in your team?" only if you're not sure they're the hiring manager. If the role page lists them as the lead, drop the fallback — it weakens the email.

## Anti-patterns — DO NOT WRITE

Same forbidden phrases as the recruiter prompt: no "I'd love", "passionate", "rockstar", "huge fan", filler openers, false urgency, embedded source ids, untrue flattery. ALSO:
- Do NOT call yourself "ideal candidate", "perfect fit", or "best for the role". Those are claims for the hiring manager to make, not you.
- Do NOT promise outcomes you can't back ("I can reduce your incident response time by 30%"). Describe the deliverable, let them infer the value.
- Do NOT lead with the candidate's degree or year — lead with the work product. Education context comes later, briefly.
