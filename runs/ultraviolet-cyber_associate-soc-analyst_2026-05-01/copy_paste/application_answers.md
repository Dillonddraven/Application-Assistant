# Application Answers

### How has your education and project work prepared you for this entry-level role?
My coursework targeted what entry-level analysts actually do day-to-day. In an incident-response course I built a playbook that started from a business impact analysis, identified sales as the client's essential function and sequenced infrastructure-restoration steps so sales-supporting systems came back online first. I also designed a tiered cloud backup schedule that placed frequently accessed data in fast-retrieval storage and infrequently accessed data in cheaper archival tiers, balancing recovery time against ongoing cost. Combined with my Dillard's internship work on SAST ingestion and vulnerability reporting, my Graylog/OpenSearch centralized logging lab (rsyslog and NXLog forwarding with sudo/auth and failed-logon alerts), Wireshark-based traffic analysis from the Accurate Interpretations role, and CMMC v2 / NIST SP 800-171 documentation work, I'm coming in ready to monitor events, triage findings, and escalate when needed.

### Describe your experience with security logs or SIEM platforms.
In a secure systems administration lab, I configured log forwarding from Linux and Windows endpoints into a centralized logging environment. This gave me hands-on experience validating event ingestion, reviewing authentication activity, and troubleshooting cases where expected events were not appearing. I also built a Python script to classify log entries into clearer categories, which improved visibility into system activity and made it easier to identify security-relevant patterns. That experience helped me understand the full monitoring workflow: generating events, forwarding logs, validating collection, classifying activity, and using the results to support investigation.

### Describe your experience with vulnerability assessment and reporting.
I have direct experience turning vulnerability data into remediation guidance. During my information security internship, I helped build a SAST reporting workflow that ingested findings from company GitLab repositories and converted raw scan output into clean Excel reports with severity breakdowns, team/repository views, and visual summaries. The value was not just collecting vulnerabilities, it was making them understandable. The reports gave stakeholders a clearer view into active vulnerabilities across GitLab repos and helped them prioritize remediation based on severity, ownership, and visibility instead of sorting through raw scanner output.
